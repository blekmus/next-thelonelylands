A weirdly detailed dream.

I ascended the stairs, my heart beating rapidly as sweat rolled down my neck as I carefully placed one foot in front of the other. The dimly lit neon corridor stretched out before me, casting shadows of strange heights. As if the rugged cement walls weren't suffocating enough, the people beside me, filling the hallway to the brim, certainly were.

Of course, this wasn't a lone journey. Surrounded by children, I was the only adult in the train of people crawling up the stairs, towering over even the eldest by a significant margin. Yet despite their youth, they seemed eerily silent. A strange contrast to the chatter and noise typically associated with youngsters.

As we climbed higher, I heard a few voices break through the quiet. They weren't speaking to convey anything meaningful; rather, the sounds of their words seemed to comfort them as they echoed through the cold and desolate space. I couldn't help but wonder what these children were doing here in this forbidding place, but I pushed the thought aside, focusing on the steps beneath my feet.

The building itself was shaped like a pipe, with the corridor winding around it like an anaconda. There were no windows and there was hardly a door either. I knew it went well below ground level, but I had never dared to venture down there.  I only hung around the lower levels because I always felt safe here.

As the smell of ale grew stronger with each step, I knew I was getting closer to my destination. A bright light emerging from a door in the swirling corridor, illuminating everything in its vicinity marked my workplace. The Pub.

The Pub specialized in three things and three things only; alcohol, guns and knives. It was a strange combination, to be sure, but it seemed to work for the clientele. After heading behind the counter. I looked back. From my vantage point, I could see a steady stream of children going up to the upper floors. They reminded me of ants scurrying into a hill, seeking shelter from an impending storm.

As I prepped myself for my first customers of the day, I couldn't help but think how unusual The Pub was. It looked more like a weapons foundry than a place to chill out. And yet, despite the danger that hung in the air like a heavy fog, the children sought refuge in the numbing embrace of alcohol. It was a way for them to drown out the world outside and its myriad troubles, if only for a little while. The sound of glass clinking and hushed conversations that usually filled the air, created a strangely comforting ambience.

Despite the oddness of it all, I found myself drawn to the place. There was something fascinating about the mix of innocence and danger, of children and guns, of a world within a world. 

I wondered what would happen if someone from the outside stumbled upon this place. Would they be horrified? Intrigued? Perhaps they would be drawn in, just like I was.

As the children came and went. Their presence a constant reminder of the strange and surreal nature of this place. And yet, in a way, they gave me hope. If they could survive in a place like this, then maybe there was hope for the rest of us.

For now, though, I was content to tend to the needs of the customers, pouring drinks and listening to their lament. Even though The Pub was supposed to be a smithing workshop, as the name suggests, the alcoholic beverages that started off as a gimmick quickly became the main attraction. 

As always, the sound of little steps walking in from the crowded hallway signaled the beginning of another day behind the think wooden counter. The familiar smell of cheap beer and stale cigarettes filled the air as the regulars made their way in, taking their usual seats and greeting me with nods and half-hearted smiles. 

Amidst the buzz of conversation, my bar mate Kasun was already dicking around, not giving a crap about our customers. He was the weapons guy, after all. All he cared about were knives and guns. Everything else was irrelevant to him, even the clients who were responsible for his salary.

But then, something caught my attention. A knife was placed on the counter, like so many others before it. a young boy of about twelve years old stood before it, looking around for assistance. I could see his eyes darting around, searching for someone to help him. Seeing his confusion, without a moments hesitation, I stepped out from behind the counter and asked him what he needed.

The boy's face lit up with excitement as he explained that he needed his knife sharpened and some modifications made. Although I knew nothing about knives, I had learned a thing or two from my experience in the bar. So I listened intently and carefully took down his requests.

After hearing him out, I turned to Kasun, who was engrossed in a conversation with a couple of beer chuggers and had not even noticed the young customer.

I felt a surge of anger, but I suppressed it, knowing that this was how Kasun always was. So I marched up to him, grabbed him by the collar and dragged him over to his workbench. He complained as if I had cut off his final words from his death bed. But now, I had his full attention.

I took this moment of clarity to show him the knife. He took one look as it and his eyes widened in surprise. This wasn't the kind of blade that was usually made after the Outside became what it is. Back then, knives were a commodity, something people carried to look cool. There were small pocket knives that would open up with the push of a button, and there were ones that had elaborate sheathes. They were considered tools of pride.

However, in today's world, having a good knife that could slice was the difference between life and death. Everyone carried one, and most were standard-issue daggers that were cheap to make. But as the world outside became increasingly inhospitable, and mining became harder and harder, even the lower quality knives became more expensive.

As I handed the knife to Kasun, I couldn't help but feel a sense of sadness for the world we now lived in. The Outside had become a dangerous place, and everyone had to be prepared to defend themselves at any moment. It was a stark contrast to the world I had grown up in, where knives were just tools of pride and not instruments of survival. As I looked at the young boy's knife, I wondered how much more dangerous the world had become.

All the while Kasun looked at the knife closely, examining the sheathe as if it was a priceless gem. His fingers traced the intricate designs on the hilt, and he nodded in appreciation. "This is a good one," he said, finally breaking the silence. "Real good."

The knife was a curious oddity. It seemed to defy logic, with a thick handle and a thin blade, almost as if it had been designed by someone with a twisted sense of humour. The dark metal casing that wrapped around it was almost as long as a man's hand, and just as wide as four fingers pressed together. Two outer blades were nestled on either side of the metal casing, perhaps replacements for the main blade that lay in the middle. The outer blades were thinner than the inner ones, and it seemed almost as if they were an afterthought, hastily added to the design.

Kasun's eyes flicked over the knife with a practised eye. He was a man who knew his blades, having spent most of his life tinkering with them. "I can fix this," he said, his voice low and confident. "It's going to take some work, but I can do it."

I nodded, my gaze flicking up towards the customer who had handed us the knife. But the young man was already gone, lost in the throng of people that were making their way up the stairs towards the children's quarters. It was a busy day, and everyone had a job to do.

I turned back towards Kasun, watching as he carefully unsheathed the blade from the intricate casing. The metal glinted in the light, catching my eye. It was a good blade, despite its odd design. And it was clear that the young man who had brought it to us valued it greatly.

I read aloud the requests I had written down and Kasun set to work. His fingers moving deftly over the blade as he assessed the damage. He was a master at what he did, and I watched with a sense of awe as he worked. It was like watching a magician at work, his hands moving in a blur as he worked his magic.

But as he worked, my mind kept drifting towards the young man who had disappeared into the crowd. I wondered what his job was, and how important it must have been to him. In this place, all jobs were considered equal, and there was no occupational discrimination. With the limited staff we had running the place, we couldn't afford to place anyone or any job in indiscreetly marked boxes of prejudice. We were all just cogs in a machine, working together to keep things running smoothly.

As I stood there, lost in my thoughts, I suddenly felt a hand on my shoulder. It was Kasun. I turned to look at him, and I saw that his gaze was fixed on the crowd in front of us. For a brief moment, he stood there silently, taking in the scene before us. Then, with a sigh, he spoke.

"This blade needs a replacement," he said. "They're a rare bunch, and we don't have them here. They're in the armoury, two floors down."

I could see the lazy discontent forming on his face, and I knew that he was not happy with this turn of events. Truth be told, I wasn't feeling particularly thrilled about it either. But I was better at hiding my emotions than Kasun, so I kept my face blank and nodded in agreement. "Let's go," I said.

Kasun pointed to the crowd crawling up the stairs, and I could see the doubt in his eyes. "Through this mess of people?" he asked, incredulous. I looked at the swarm of bodies before us and felt my resolve waver. But then I remembered why we were here, and I knew that we couldn't run from our responsibilities. "No matter," I said firmly. "We'll make it through. We have to."

And so we began our descent down the two flights of stairs, pushing our way through the storming children. It was chaos all around us, with voices shouting, feet stamping, and bodies jostling for space. I could feel the heat rising in my face, and I knew that Kasun was feeling it too. But we kept our heads down and pressed on, determined to reach our goal.

As we neared the bottom of the stairs, we caught sight of one or two guides. They were the only other adults here, other than me and Kasun, whose job was to make sure the group of children they were assigned made it up the tower. I could see the weariness etched on their faces, and I knew that they had been dealing with this chaos for hours.

We approached them, and Kasun spoke up. "Excuse me," he said. "We need to get to the armoury. Do you know the way?"

The guides looked at us warily, sizing us up. But then they must have seen the determination in our eyes, for they nodded and pointed to a door at the end of the hallway.

"That way," one of them said. "But be careful. It's dangerous down there."

With that warning ringing in our ears, we set off towards the door. We could hear the sound of clashing swords and the shouts of warriors echoing from the other side. I felt a thrill of fear and excitement run through me, knowing that we were getting closer to our destination.

As we pushed open the door, we were met with a sight that took our breath away. The armoury was a vast room, filled with racks of weapons and armor of all shapes and sizes. There were swords, shields, helmets, and breastplates as far as the eye could see. I could feel my heart pounding in my chest, and I knew that Kasun felt the same.

We made our way through the maze of racks, searching for the rare blade that we needed. It took us a while, but finally, we found it - a gleaming silver blade, with a sharp edge and a perfect balance.

Kasun took it in his hand, and I could see the satisfaction in his eyes. "This is it," he said. "This is the one we need."

I nodded, and we made our way back through the maze of racks, our hearts pounding with excitement.
